Methodology

How ONTO Measures Epistemic Risk

Deterministic scoring grounded in information theory, complexity theory, and formal epistemology. Every metric defined. Every formula published. Every result verifiable.

Foundational Frameworks
8 established theoretical bases
01
Shannon Entropy
Shannon, 1948
Quantifies informational uncertainty. ONTO uses Shannon entropy to measure the information content of model outputs and calibration distributions.
02
Kolmogorov Complexity
Kolmogorov, 1965 · Li & Vitányi, 2019
Measures algorithmic information content. The IGR formula uses Kolmogorov complexity to assess the gap between claimed and supported knowledge.
03
Eigen Error Threshold
Eigen, 1971
Minimum accuracy for information preservation across copies. Applied to AI: below the threshold, output quality degrades in chain-of-thought and multi-agent pipelines.
04
Landauer's Principle
Landauer, 1961
Physical lower bound on information erasure. Establishes that information processing has irreducible thermodynamic costs — information cannot be created from noise.
05
Dissipative Structures
Prigogine, 1977
Self-organization in open systems. Defines the ceiling of spontaneous order — the maximum complexity that can emerge without external information input.
06
Formal Epistemology
Epistemic Logic tradition
Logical framework for knowledge, belief, and justification. Grounds the EM1-EM5 taxonomy of epistemic markers in formal distinction between knowledge states.
07
Calibration Theory
Dawid, 1982 · DeGroot & Fienberg, 1983
Statistical theory of well-calibrated forecasters. Forms the mathematical basis for EpCE — measuring whether expressed confidence matches actual accuracy.
08
Information Gap Ratio (IGR)
ONTO Standards Council, 2024
Normalized metric for epistemic gap assessment. IGR(E,S) = max(0, 1 − H_max(S)/K(E)). When IGR → 1, the system claims far beyond its informational support.
Core Formula
Information Gap Ratio
Definition 1 — IGR
IGR(E, S) = max(0, 1 − Hmax(S) / K(E))

Where K(E) is the Kolmogorov complexity of the claimed content and Hmax(S) is the maximum Shannon entropy the system can reliably encode for the domain. When IGR ≈ 0, the system's capacity is sufficient. When IGR → 1, the system is producing claims far beyond its informational support.

Direct computation of K(E) is uncomputable. ONTO operationalizes the framework through three proxy metrics that are deterministic, reproducible, and empirically validated.

Three Metrics
Linguistic · Calibration · Agreement
REP
Response Epistemic Profile
Measures the distribution of epistemic markers (EM1–EM5) in model output. REP = 0: full transparency. REP = 1: complete overclaiming. What the model says about its certainty.
EpCE
Epistemic Calibration Error
Quantifies the gap between expressed confidence and factual accuracy. Bridge formula connecting linguistic layer to factual verification. What the model actually knows.
DLA
Dual-Layer Agreement
Detects discrepancies between linguistic self-assessment and statistical behavior. DLA ≈ 1: layers agree. DLA ≪ 1: dangerous — confident language, wrong facts.
Composite Risk Score
risk(R) = ω₁ · REP(R) + ω₂ · EpCE(R) + ω₃ · (1 − DLA(R))
where ω₁ + ω₂ + ω₃ = 1, weights domain-calibrated against GOLD v4.5

The Dual-Layer Insight
Why ONTO is different

Existing calibration metrics measure the gap between confidence and accuracy at the logit level — the model's internal probability distributions. ONTO measures this gap at the linguistic output level: what the model says about its certainty versus what is actually true.

A model can be well-calibrated at the logit level while being poorly calibrated at the linguistic level — expressing certainty in its text despite low internal confidence. DLA detects exactly this discrepancy.

The key insight: epistemic risk is not "is this answer correct?" It is "does this model know what it doesn't know?" A model that says "I'm not sure" when it's not sure is safer than a model that says "definitely" when it's guessing.

GOLD Calibration Standard
v4.5 · Reference corpus

All scoring is calibrated against the GOLD (Ground-truth Ontology for Linguistic Diagnostics) reference corpus. GOLD provides:

· Calibration probes (controlled inputs with known correct responses)
· Domain-specific TCI baselines (expected accuracy per domain)
· EM pattern validation (verified epistemic marker classifications)
· Reference implementation of scoring formulas

GOLD ensures that scores are reproducible, deterministic, and calibrated against a stable reference — not trained on shifting benchmarks.
